A Crisis in Healthcare for Rural America

Rural hospitals are closing; many more are in danger.

By Ezra Nielsen | Dec. 18, 2024

Imagine you’re having a heart attack. Now imagine you’re hours away from a hospital. This is the situation a growing number of Americans who live in rural areas face. And data suggests the situation is only going to worsen.

Since 2005, 194 rural hospitals have closed in the U.S. Another 602 hospitals are at immediate risk of closing, greatly widening the healthcare deserts that already exist in the system.

Zohn Rosen, a lecturer at the Columbia University Mailman School of Public Health, said there are a number of factors that cause rural hospital closures: it’s hard to get doctors to move to rural areas, hospitals need to have a constant flow of patients, which is not the case in rural areas because of population density, and hospitals run on a razor-thin 1% to 1.5% margin of profit.

“So you have these really big holes in the market where you have people who are high need and no access,” Rosen said.

According to him, there’s only one solution to the problem. “If you want to provide healthcare, the government’s going to have to pay one way or another,” he said. But the incoming Trump administration, he added, wants to squeeze money out of the system, not add to it.
